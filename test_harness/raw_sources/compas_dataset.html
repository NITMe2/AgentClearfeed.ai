COMPAS Recidivism Dataset

📥 124,567 downloads ⭐ 4.8/5 (892 ratings) 🔄 Last verified: April 2026 📄 License: CC-BY-4.0

About This Dataset

The COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) dataset became one of the most important datasets in algorithmic fairness research following ProPublica's groundbreaking 2016 investigation, "Machine Bias."

ProPublica obtained risk scores for over 7,000 defendants arrested in Broward County, Florida during 2013-2014 through a public records request. They then tracked which defendants were charged with new crimes over the next two years to evaluate the accuracy and fairness of the COMPAS algorithm.

Key Findings from ProPublica's Analysis

The investigation revealed significant racial disparities in the algorithm's error rates:

  • Black defendants were almost twice as likely as white defendants to be labeled a higher risk but not actually re-offend
  • White defendants who did re-offend were much more likely than Black defendants to have been labeled lower risk
  • The algorithm's overall accuracy was only about 60%, roughly equivalent to predictions made by people with no criminal justice expertise

Known Biases and Limitations

⚠️ Important Considerations

  • Single Jurisdiction: Data is from Broward County, Florida only and may not generalize to other jurisdictions
  • Limited Follow-up: Two-year recidivism window may undercount actual reoffending
  • Proxy Measures: Uses re-arrest as a proxy for re-offending, which conflates criminal behavior with policing patterns
  • Proprietary Algorithm: The COMPAS scoring methodology is proprietary and not fully disclosed by Northpointe (now Equivant)
  • Racial Disparities in Error Rates: Black defendants had significantly higher false positive rates

Recommended Uses

This dataset is appropriate for:

  • Fairness metric benchmarking and comparison
  • Bias auditing methodology development
  • Educational purposes in algorithmic fairness courses
  • Evaluating debiasing algorithms

Not Recommended For

  • Production recidivism prediction systems
  • Generalizing to jurisdictions outside the original study
  • Drawing conclusions about current criminal justice practices
