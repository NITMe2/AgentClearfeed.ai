The Impossibility Theorem of Fairness: Why You Can't Have It All

Two landmark papers proved that the most popular fairness metrics are mathematically incompatible. Here's what that means for practitioners.

Dr. James Wright May 3, 2026 12 min read

In the world of algorithmic fairness, there's a result so fundamental that it shapes every serious conversation about fair machine learning. It's often called the "impossibility theorem," and once you understand it, you'll see why the debate about COMPAS, hiring algorithms, and lending models can never be resolved by better technology alone.

The Setup: Three Reasonable Requirements

Imagine you're building a system that predicts whether someone will default on a loan. You want it to be fair. Here are three things that seem perfectly reasonable to ask for:

  1. Calibration: When your model says someone has a 70% chance of defaulting, they should actually default about 70% of the time — and this should hold regardless of their demographic group.
  2. Equal false positive rates: The rate at which you incorrectly flag someone as likely to default should be the same across groups. (This is part of equalised odds.)
  3. Equal false negative rates: The rate at which you miss actual defaulters should also be the same across groups. (This is the other part of equalised odds.)

Each of these seems not just reasonable but necessary. Surely any fair system should satisfy all three?

The Proof: You Can't

In 2016-2017, two independent research groups proved that these requirements are mathematically incompatible in most real-world scenarios:

  • Chouldechova (2017) showed that calibration and equal false positive/negative rates cannot both hold when the base rates (actual rates of the outcome) differ between groups.
  • Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan (2016) proved a broader version: three natural conditions on any risk score are mutually incompatible except in degenerate cases.

This isn't a limitation of current algorithms. It's not something that can be fixed with more data, better features, or smarter engineering. It's a mathematical fact — as inescapable as the Pythagorean theorem.

"The choice of which fairness metric to prioritise is a normative decision, not a technical one."

Why Base Rates Matter

The key condition is differing base rates. If Group A defaults on loans at 10% and Group B defaults at 20%, then no classifier — no matter how sophisticated — can simultaneously be calibrated and have equal error rates across both groups.

In the real world, base rates almost always differ between demographic groups, because they reflect centuries of structural inequality, differing socioeconomic conditions, and measurement biases. The impossibility theorem means these structural differences create unavoidable fairness trade-offs in any predictive system.

The COMPAS Connection

This is exactly what happened in the famous COMPAS debate. ProPublica said COMPAS was biased because Black defendants had much higher false positive rates. Northpointe said COMPAS was fair because it was calibrated — the scores meant the same thing regardless of race. Both were right. Both couldn't be satisfied. The impossibility theorem explains why.

What This Means for Practitioners

If you're building ML systems that affect people's lives, the impossibility theorem has a clear implication: you must choose which type of fairness to prioritize, and that choice is a value judgment, not a technical one.

No amount of engineering can escape this trade-off. The responsible thing is to make the choice explicitly, document it, and ensure the people affected by the system understand which definition of fairness is being applied and why.
