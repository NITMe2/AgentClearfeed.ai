Understanding Demographic Parity in Machine Learning

If you're working in machine learning, you've probably heard the term demographic parity thrown around in discussions about fairness and bias. But what does it actually mean? In this comprehensive guide, we'll break down everything you need to know about this crucial fairness metric.

What is Demographic Parity?

Demographic parity (also known as statistical parity or group fairness) is one of the most widely used definitions of fairness in machine learning. At its core, the concept is straightforward:

A model satisfies demographic parity if it produces positive predictions at equal rates across all demographic groups.

In more technical terms, a classifier satisfies demographic parity when:

P(Ŷ=1 | A=0) = P(Ŷ=1 | A=1)

Where A represents the protected attribute (such as race or gender) and Ŷ represents the model's prediction.

Why Does Demographic Parity Matter?

In many high-stakes decision-making contexts — hiring, lending, criminal justice — we want to ensure that our algorithms don't systematically favor one group over another. Demographic parity provides a simple, measurable criterion for checking this.

For example, if a hiring algorithm selects 30% of male applicants for interviews, demographic parity would require it to also select approximately 30% of female applicants.

💡 Key Takeaway

Demographic parity focuses on outcomes, not process. It doesn't care how the model makes decisions — only that the results are balanced across groups.

Common Misconceptions

One of the most important things to understand about demographic parity is what it does not guarantee. A model can satisfy demographic parity while being significantly less accurate for one group than another. Equal prediction rates don't mean equal error rates.

This is directly related to the famous impossibility theorem by Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg et al. (2016), which proved that demographic parity, calibration, and equalised odds cannot all be satisfied simultaneously except in trivial cases.

Related Concepts

Understanding demographic parity is easier when you know how it relates to other fairness metrics:

  • Equalised Odds — requires equal true positive and false positive rates across groups
  • Individual Fairness — requires similar individuals to receive similar predictions
  • Calibration — requires predicted probabilities to match actual outcomes across groups
  • Disparate Impact — the legal framework for measuring disproportionate outcomes

The Bottom Line

Demographic parity remains one of the most commonly cited group fairness metrics, but it's crucial to understand its limitations. The choice of which fairness metric to optimise for is ultimately a value judgement, not a purely technical decision.

As Barocas, Hardt, and Narayanan note in their influential textbook Fairness and Machine Learning: "Statistical parity requires that the proportion of people receiving a positive outcome is the same in each group."
